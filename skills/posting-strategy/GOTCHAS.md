# Gotchas — Posting Strategy

## 1. "Best Posting Times" Are Averages, Not Your Data

**What happens:** Agent recommends posting at "9 AM Tuesday" because aggregate data says it's the best time. But the user's audience is in a different timezone, or their specific niche has different activity patterns.

**Why it matters:** Published "best times" are cross-industry averages. A B2B SaaS founder targeting CTOs has different audience activity patterns than a DTC beauty brand targeting Gen Z. The difference between optimal and average timing can be 2-3x in engagement.

**How to avoid:** Use published timing data as a starting point, then adjust based on the user's actual analytics. If they have platform analytics, use THEIR audience's active hours. Test different times for 2-3 weeks and measure.

## 2. The LinkedIn Post Collision

**What happens:** Calendar schedules two LinkedIn posts in 24 hours. The second post kills the first post's momentum because LinkedIn's algorithm makes them compete against each other.

**The data:** Richard van der Blom's analysis (Jan 2026) showed posting twice within 24 hours consistently reduced reach on LinkedIn. Under 360Brew, posts from the same account compete for the same audience's attention. Wait at least 24 hours between LinkedIn posts.

**How to avoid:** LinkedIn: never more than 1 post per 24 hours. If a post is still gaining traction (check engagement 12-18 hours later), delay the next one. Under 360Brew, late engagement (24-72h) can drive 4-6x more reach — don't kill it with a premature follow-up.

## 3. Cross-Platform Flooding

**What happens:** Agent schedules the same core idea across all 5 platforms at the same time. Followers who follow the user on multiple platforms see the same thing 5 times in one morning.

**The data:** Platform audiences overlap. A Socialync cross-posting study (Jan 2026) found optimal stagger is: primary platform first → secondary 1-2 days later → tertiary 1 week later. Same-day cross-posting isn't penalized by the algorithms (platforms can't detect it), but it fatigues your multi-platform followers.

**How to avoid:** Space out cross-platform posting by at least a few hours. Ideally stagger by 1-2 days. Use the cross-posting sequence: discovery platforms first (TikTok/Reels), conversation second (X/Reddit), authority third (LinkedIn/YouTube).

## 4. Posting 5 Days Then Going Dark

**What happens:** Founder posts every day for a week, then goes silent for 2 weeks because they got busy. Repeats this cycle.

**Why it matters:** Every platform's algorithm rewards consistency. TikTok's January 2026 update specifically punishes inconsistent posting — it tests your content with followers first, and if your followers aren't used to seeing you, they don't engage, and the video dies. LinkedIn's 360Brew takes ~90 days of consistent posting to fully categorize you.

**How to avoid:** Set a sustainable frequency and stick to it. 3 posts/week every week is better than 7/week followed by 0/week. If the user can't sustain daily posting, recommend 3/week consistently.

## 5. Ignoring Post Lifespan Differences

**What happens:** Agent treats every platform the same — post and move on. But a TikTok video can resurface weeks later, while an X tweet effectively dies in hours. A LinkedIn post can get a 4-6x boost from saves at 72 hours.

**The data (Richard van der Blom, Jan 2026):**
- LinkedIn 2024: 20% of reach in first hour, 35% in first 24h
- LinkedIn 2026: 40% of reach in first hour, 55% in first 24h, but last 5% can come 6-8 days later

**How to avoid:** Match engagement strategy to post lifespan. X: reply to comments in first 30-60 minutes (the velocity window). LinkedIn: engage for 48+ hours (delayed engagement gets rewarded). TikTok: check back on old posts periodically (they can resurface).
